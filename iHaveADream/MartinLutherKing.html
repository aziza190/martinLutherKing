King received the Nobel Peace Prize on October 14, 1964, in recognition of his nonviolent struggle against racial injustice. He assisted in the planning of two of the three Selma to Montgomery marches in 1965. In his latter years, he broadened his focus to encompass opposition to the Vietnam War, capitalism, and poverty. When King was killed on April 4 in Memphis, Tennessee, he was preparing a national occupation of Washington, D.C., to be known as the Poor People's Campaign. After his passing, there was widespread sadness throughout the country as well as rage that sparked riots in numerous American cities. King received the Congressional Gold Medal in 2003 and the Presidential Medal of Freedom posthumously in 1977.

Martin Luther King and his Contributions to society on becoming a historical figure The Southern Christian Leadership Conference was established in 1957 by King, Ralph Abernathy, Fred Shuttlesworth, Joseph Lowery, and other civil rights leaders (SCLC). The organization was founded in order to use the moral weight and mobilizing capacity of black churches to lead peaceful demonstrations in support of civil rights legislation.The SCLC launched A campaign in Birmingham, Alabama, against racial segregation and economic injustice in April 1963. Wyatt Tee Walker helped create some of the peaceful but purposefully provocative strategies that were used in the campaign. In Birmingham, black people who were organizing with the SCLC occupied public venues with marches and sit-ins, openly flouting the law because they believed it to be unfair.

King, representing the SCLC, was among the leaders of the "Big Six" civil rights organizations who were instrumental in the organization of the March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom, which took place on August 28, 1963.
